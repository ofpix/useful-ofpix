《律路街上的林家》是一个对居住在纽约唐人街的中国家庭的生活调查。在纽约长大的我发现大多数人都把唐人街简单视作一个旅游景点和周末去吃吃点心，买买便宜货的地方。虽然我自己从没在中国城生活过，但我的祖父母却生活在那里，我也知道唐人街比那些旅游宣传册和明信片中老套的形象要丰富得多。我自己也是半个中国人，也花过很长时间晃在唐人街里，但我从未感觉我和那里的文化连在一起——我总是一个来做客的人。在这个项目开始时，我只是想走进那些关着的门后面，拍摄到老套的唐人街影像之外的东西。我想感受唐人街里人们的日常。
在参加一个本地住房支持公益项目的几个月里，我在一次家访中遇到了姓林的这家人。后来，我又返回了几次林家并且拍了照片，雪莉和史蒂芬总是非常友好，并欢迎我到他们家。我越来越多地开始参与他们的日常家庭生活和意识到他们的家庭内部动态的关系。我去参加婚礼，帮忙食品购物，去孩子们的学校，并最终前往中国大陆和香港探望他们的亲戚。我开始感觉像家人一样。
多年来，我一直随身带着一台宝丽来相机。我一直在收集三个林家的孩子用我的相机拍摄的宝丽来照片，并存放在一个盒子里。一段时间后，我重新审视他们的宝丽来照片，立刻为他们眼中的影像和我多么不同而震惊。孩子们的照片在事实的直接和观点表达上十分有力。我还记得他们所有照片的拍摄时间，我很好奇我们看到同一个现实时按下的快门、拍摄的照片有何不同。我意识到不同的叙述正在同时发生着，他们的作品和我的作品反映了这一事实。我决定，通过一同展示我的与他们的作品，通过探索存在于我们的同一个现实中的多个叙事的想法，我可以创造一个更令人信服的故事。通过我们的照片都看到合并后的叙述。当记录我与他们相遇，或说他们与我的相遇时，我想观众能更好的感受到林家的世界。
即使自从15岁起我就以某种方式与摄影连在一起，我万万没有想到会如此接近和在感情上依赖我的“被摄物”。我现在明白了，我们是谁不是因为一些社会的刻板印象而是因为我们自己独特的历史和经历。我们生活在一个复杂的文化世界（尤其是纽约市），不同的文化有意无意间混合和相互影响着。不存在全球通用的人生规划了。我是谁是因为我的家庭的历史，而林家的人们是谁则是因为他们的历史。当我更接近林家，我认出了他们生活中我也在分享着的元素。但我将有可能知道做一个“中国人”意味着什么吗？答案是否定的。但我可以尝试了解一个单一的中国家庭（或任何国籍）可能如何生活吗？答案是肯定的。我没有办法通过简单的拍摄林家来回答我自己的个人问题。不过，我发现了一个，我从来没有料想到的，更大的关于人类本性的理解。当我开始理解我从未接触过的唐人街家庭时，有关我一半中国血统的问题最终在一个和我最初设想完全不同的经历中被回答了。
“The Lams of Ludlow Street” is an investigation into the life of one Chinese family living in New York City’s Chinatown neighborhood. Growing up in New York City I have watched most people regard Chinatown simply as a tourist attraction and a weekend destination for dim sum and cheap counterfeit goods. While I have never lived in Chinatown, my grandparents do, and I knew that Chinatown was more than the stereotypical images seen in travel brochures and postcards. Although I am half-Chinese and have spent a considerable amount if time in Chinatown, I never experienced a bond or connection to the neighborhood or the culture; I always felt I was a visitor. At the beginning of this project, I knew I wanted to get behind closed doors and photograph more than the stereotypical images of Chinatown’s street scenes. I wanted to experience more of the daily life of Chinatown.
While accompanying a local housing advocate for several months, I met the Lams on one of our home visits. I returned several times to the Lams’ home with photographs, and Shirley and Steven were always very friendly and welcomed me into their home. I increasingly began to participate in their everyday family rituals and to recognize the dynamics and relationships within their family. I went to weddings, helped with the food shopping, went to the children’s school and eventually traveled to China and Hong Kong to visit their relatives. I began to feel like family.
For years, I have always carried a Polaroid Spectra instant camera with me. For over a year I had collected the Polaroid photographs that the three Lam children had taken with my camera and simply stored them in a box. After some time, I finally revisited their Polaroids. I was immediately struck by how different their images were from mine. The children’s images were very powerful in their matter-of-fact directness and point of view. I could recall when all of their pictures were taken, and I became interested in how we both had different takes on one shared reality. I realized that there were different narratives simultaneously occurring and that their work and my work reflected this fact. I decided that by showing my work alongside theirs I could create a more compelling narrative by exploring the idea of multiple narratives existing within our shared reality. By seeing the combined narrative of both our photographs, I want the viewer to get a better sense of the Lams’ world while documenting my encounters with them and vice versa.
Even though I have been involved with photography in some way since the age of fifteen, I never expected to become so close and emotionally tied to my “subject matter.” I now understand that we are who we are not because of some socially constructed stereotype but because of our personally unique histories and experiences. We live in a culturally complex world (especially New York City) where cultures mix and influence one another either subconsciously or deliberately. There are no universal life plans. I am who I am because of my family history, while the Lams are who they are. As I became closer to the Lams, I recognized elements in their lives that I shared. Will I ever know what it means to be “Chinese?” The answer is no. But can I attempt to understand how a single Chinese family (or any nationality) might live? The answer is yes. There is no way that by simply photographing the Lams I could ever answer my own personal questions. However I discovered a greater understanding about human nature that I never expected. The questions I have always had regarding my Chinese half were ultimately addressed in a completely different experience that I originally envisioned as I began to understand the Chinatown family I never had.
